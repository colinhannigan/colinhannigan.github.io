POLS 222: Analysis: U.S. Policy/Election

Last Taught: Spring 2020

What makes the field of Political Science a science? How do scholars corroborate their theories? How can you strengthen your own arguments using empirical evidence? The goal of this course is to introduce students to the scientific process used by political scientists.

The interpretation and analysis of data is essential for almost any career that a political science major might pursue. Lawyers and lobbyists, politicians, and professors all need to be able to read and understand reports in which numerical summaries of data are used as evidence to support an argument or point of view. These professionals need to ascertain whether such statistics are being used appropriately, and how they are best interpreted. Sometimes professionals need to do their own statistical analysis. This course introduces students to social science research methods and the scientific study of politics primarily through a light-on-the-math approach to statistical models. Students will learn why data are useful, how to interpret a variety of statistics, and how to analyze evidence systematically to generate their own findings. Students will also learn how to apply the scientific research process to their own research questions by completing a research design project.

This class will often rely on examples and evidence from a U.S. political perspective (particularly in terms of behavior, attitudes, elections, and policy) or a global perspective, but the logic and methodology introduced here are applicable to scientific inquiry in a variety of disciplines. As we explore the methods through which political scientists make sense of the social world, students will be encouraged to think critically about politics and their roles as participants within it.
